From Pixels to Diagnosis: Using Machine Learning to Classify Medical Image Sequences

Antonio Badilla-Olivas, Enrique Vilchez-Lizano, Brandon Mora-Umaña, Kenneth Villalobos-Solís, Adrián Lara Petitdemange
Universidad de Costa Rica · Escuela de Ciencias de la Computación e Informática
View code Dataset

The question

When a patient arrives with a traumatic brain injury, clinicians rely on CT scans to detect intracranial hemorrhage. Each scan produces around 30 slices. A radiologist reads them as a sequence. Can machine learning do the same?

We wanted to test a specific idea: does treating CT slices as a video sequence (rather than individual images) improve classification? And if so, why?

Two approaches

We compared two models on the same 82 patients from the CT-ICH dataset, 36 with hemorrhage and 46 without.

ViViT is a video vision transformer. It takes 18 CT slices as a single sequence and outputs one prediction per patient. It sees the data at the patient level, where the class balance is roughly 1:1.1.

ConvNeXT is a convolutional image model. It classifies each slice independently, then applies a voting rule: if any slice is positive, the patient is positive. It sees the data at the slice level, where approximately 90% of samples are negative.

The imbalance problem

This is the core finding. The two models don't just differ in architecture. They face fundamentally different training distributions from the same data.

ConvNeXT

ConvNeXT sees roughly 2,800 individual slices, 90% of which are negative. It learns to predict "no hemorrhage" almost every time. High accuracy, terrible recall. ViViT sees 75 patient-level sequences with near-balanced classes. It actually learns to detect hemorrhage.

This imbalance is not a flaw in the experiment. It is what happens when you force an image model to process sequential data. The decomposition into individual frames creates the skew.

ViViT produced less than half the false negatives of ConvNeXT and detected positive cases more than four times as often. In a medical context, where a missed hemorrhage can be fatal, this matters.

ViViT is a larger model with lower throughput (it processes full sequences), but total training time is comparable. Both were trained on a Tesla V100S with 10 replicates of 20 epochs each.

Limitations

  • 82 patients from a single hospital. Results may not generalise.
  • One architecture per model category. We can't claim all video models beat all image models.
  • No class-balancing techniques were applied. This was deliberate (we wanted to compare paradigms under natural conditions), but it means ConvNeXT was at a disadvantage.
  • Binary classification only. The dataset has hemorrhage subtype labels that we did not use.

What comes next

Several directions are worth exploring:

  • Do class-balancing techniques (oversampling, augmentation, weighted loss) close the gap? If so, the advantage is about class distribution, not architecture.
  • How do other video models and multi-view models compare?
  • Can these models handle multiclass hemorrhage subtype classification?
  • Larger, multi-site datasets would test generalisability.
